## The title tag: the most impactful on-page element after content

The `<title>` tag is the HTML element that defines a page's title. It appears in search results, in the browser tab, and in social media shares. It's also one of the strongest on-page ranking signals in SEO.

Yet simple mistakes — title too long, missing keyword, duplicated across pages — remain extremely common.

---

## The rules for an optimized title tag

### Length: think in pixels, not characters

Google displays titles in a space of approximately **600 pixels**. Beyond that, the title is truncated with "…". The recommended length is **500 to 590 pixels**, which corresponds to roughly 55–65 characters depending on the title composition.

The catch: two titles of 60 characters can have very different widths. A "w" is twice as wide as an "i". That's why measuring in pixels is more reliable than character counting.

### Primary keyword at the beginning

Search engines give more weight to words appearing at the start of the title. Always place your primary keyword within the first 50 pixels of your title.

**Bad:** "Your ultimate guide to mastering search engine optimization techniques | OnPageLab"
**Good:** "On-Page SEO: Complete Guide to Optimize Your Pages | OnPageLab"

### Recommended title structure

The most effective structure: **[Keyword] [Angle] [Brand]**

- `Keyword`: the primary term being targeted
- `Angle`: qualifier, promise, date, number ("complete guide", "2026", "in 5 steps")
- `Brand`: site name after a separator (`|`, `—`, `·`)

Examples:
- *"On-Page SEO Audit: 6-Step Complete Method | OnPageLab"*
- *"Keyword Density: The Real Impact on SEO Rankings (2026)"*
- *"H1 Tag SEO: Complete Guide and Best Practices | OnPageLab"*

### One unique title per page

Duplicate titles across multiple pages send a negative signal. Every page on your site must have a title that's unique to it and accurately reflects its content.

## 7 title tag mistakes to avoid

1. **Missing title** — Google auto-generates a title, often irrelevant
2. **Too long** — Truncated in SERPs. Optimize for the first 590 pixels
3. **Too short** — Wasted space. A 3-word title doesn't exploit semantic potential
4. **Missing keyword** — A title without the target term misses a key relevance signal
5. **Duplicate titles** — Two pages with the same title create indexing confusion
6. **Keyword stuffing** — Repeating the keyword 3 times: "SEO | SEO Guide | Best SEO Tips" — counterproductive
7. **No differentiator** — A generic title doesn't invite clicks. Add value: number, quality, benefit

## Does Google rewrite title tags?

Yes, frequently. Recent studies show Google rewrites the title in **50 to 60% of cases**. It does so mainly when:

- The title is too long or too short
- It's considered over-optimized
- It doesn't match the page content
- A better formulation exists in the page (H1, start of article)

**How to minimize rewrites:** Write a precise title, aligned with the H1 and content, without over-optimization. A well-crafted title gets rewritten less often.

## FAQ on title tag SEO

**What's the difference between the title tag and the H1?**
The `<title>` appears in the browser tab and SERPs but not on the page itself. The `<h1>` is the visible title on the page but not in the SERPs. They serve complementary roles and should be different.

**Should I put the brand name in every title?**
Recommended for brand recognition, but not mandatory. On highly competitive pages or when the title is near the pixel limit, you can omit it.

**Should I include the year in the title?**
For regularly updated informational content (guides, rankings), yes: the year improves CTR as users prefer recent content. Remember to update it each year.
