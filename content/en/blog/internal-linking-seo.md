## Internal linking: the underrated SEO lever

In the SEO priority hierarchy, internal linking is often neglected in favor of content and link building. That's a mistake: smart internal linking can move existing pages up in rankings without writing a single new word of content.

By intelligently distributing authority between your pages, guiding Google's bots through your architecture and retaining visitors, internal linking is an SEO effectiveness multiplier.

---

## What is internal linking?

Internal linking refers to all **hyperlinks between pages on the same website**. An internal link goes from one of your pages to another page on your site.

It's distinct from:
- **External links** (outbound links): links to third-party sites
- **Backlinks** (inbound links): links from third-party sites to your site

---

## The 3 SEO roles of internal linking

### 1. PageRank distribution

Every internal link transfers a fraction of authority (PageRank) from one page to another. Pages that receive many internal links from your strong pages accumulate more internal authority — and rank better.

Strategy: identify your most authoritative pages (homepage, content pillars) and create links from them to your target pages.

### 2. Crawl facilitation

Google follows links to discover and index pages. A page with no inbound internal links is an **orphan page**: hard to find, crawled infrequently, often poorly indexed.

### 3. User retention and engagement

Relevant internal links keep visitors on your site longer, increase page views and time spent. These positive behavioral signals indirectly influence rankings.

---

## The 5 rules of effective internal linking

### Rule 1: Link by topical relevance, not randomly

Every internal link should be justified by a semantic relationship between source and target pages.

### Rule 2: Use descriptive anchor texts

❌ *"To learn more, [click here](URL)"* — generic anchor, no semantic value

✅ *"Read our complete guide on [title tag optimization](URL)"* — descriptive anchor, strong topical signal

### Rule 3: Prioritize contextual links

A link placed **in the body text** has more value than the same link in a navigation menu or footer that appears on every page. Navigation links are site-wide and diluted; contextual links are unique to the page.

### Rule 4: Build topic clusters

A **topic cluster** is a central page (usually a content pillar) that links to all cluster pages on the same theme. Clusters link back to the hub. This hub-and-spoke structure sends a strong topical authority signal.

### Rule 5: Fix orphan pages and broken links

An orphan page = no inbound internal links. Find them with a Screaming Frog crawl and create links from relevant pages.

## FAQ on internal linking

**How many internal links per page?**
No hard rule. For a blog post, 3–7 contextual links is good practice. For the homepage or hubs, more is acceptable. What matters is that every link is relevant.

**Do navigation and footer links count?**
Yes, but they're worth less than contextual links. Being site-wide, their signal is diluted. Focus your efforts on body content links.

**Should I add a return link (cluster → hub)?**
Yes. Bidirectional links (hub → cluster AND cluster → hub) reinforce topical cohesion and distribute authority in both directions.
