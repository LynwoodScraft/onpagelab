## Is keyword density still a ranking factor in 2026?

Keyword density — the percentage of a term's occurrences relative to total word count — was a central SEO factor in the early 2000s. The question is: does it still matter today?

The answer is nuanced. Raw density as an isolated metric has lost much of its significance. What matters now is **semantic coverage**: does your content treat the topic comprehensively, with the appropriate vocabulary?

---

## What keyword density to target?

The range commonly cited by SEO practitioners is **0.5% to 2%**. This is a guideline, not an absolute rule:

- **< 0.5%**: potentially too weak a relevance signal for competitive queries
- **0.5% – 2%**: natural and healthy range for most content
- **> 3%**: over-optimization risk. Can harm readability and send an artificial signal

For long content (3,000+ words), a 0.5% density may be sufficient — the absolute number of occurrences remains meaningful.

---

## How to calculate keyword density

Simple formula: `(Number of keyword occurrences / Total word count) × 100`

Example: 15 occurrences of "on-page seo" in a 1,500-word article = 1% density.

## Keyword stuffing: avoid at all costs

Keyword stuffing means excessively repeating a keyword in a text to manipulate search results. Google Panda (2011) was the first major update targeting this practice.

Signs of keyword stuffing to avoid:
- Repeating the exact keyword in every sentence
- Keyword lists without context
- Hidden keywords (white text on white background)
- Titles repeating the keyword 3 times

---

## What really counts: semantic coverage

Google doesn't count isolated keyword occurrences. It analyzes the entire content to evaluate:

- **Topical depth**: does the article cover the subject in detail?
- **Lexical diversity**: synonyms, variants, domain-specific terms
- **Named entities**: brands, people, places, related concepts
- **Structure**: is the content organized logically around the topic?

An article that mentions "on-page SEO" 15 times but never discusses title tags, meta descriptions, or internal linking will be perceived as less complete than an article covering all these aspects with just 5 occurrences of the main term.

## FAQ on keyword density

**Is there a universal ideal keyword density?**
No. It depends on content length, competition, industry and page type. The 0.5–2% range is a useful reference but not an absolute rule.

**Does density matter in all page elements?**
Body text density is most important. Presence in the title, H1 and meta description has a separate, complementary impact — not the same "density" but independent relevance signals.

**How to avoid over-optimization without losing relevance?**
Use synonyms, rephrasing and associated terms. If your text sounds repetitive when read aloud, it probably does to Google too.
